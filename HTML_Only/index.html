Re-Packaged

Introduction

Michelle Alexander, the author of the New York Times Bestseller book titled The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness, thoroughly examines the implementation of the mass incarceration system. She provides historical evidence of past marginalization and oppression of communities of color and displays how it has shifted and adapted to present-day life. Her book addresses minorities and communities of color, and while this paper will focus more specifically on black communities, she illuminates another deeply embedded process of reconstruction. In her book, Alexander shows how the United States’ deployment of mass incarceration, through legislation and the enforcement of detainment and relocation, has reconstructed the commodification of the black body. A successful business requires planning and requires both investment and return on that investment. If colored bodies are the commodity, then who are the buyers and sellers?

Legislation

Legislation has historically acted as an extremely capable and insidious scapegoat that white folks have utilized in justifying and rationalizing business ventures, decision making, and the securing of profit. Congress has passed many pieces of legislation that has not only prevented black folks opportunity and access to resources, but that has contributed towards to herding of marginalized and oppressed communities, especially black folks, into circumstances that are profitable for whites. Of a broad coverage of Congressionally passed Acts and policies created by the U.S. government, Alexander wrote about the Drug Abuse Act, initially constructed in 1986, and revisited 1988 and explores the connection to the Housing and Urban Development Department, or (HUD). (Alexander 53, 145). The Anti-Drug Abuse Act granted public housing authorities the right to evict any tenant who “allows any form of drug-related criminal activity to occur on or near public housing premises and eliminated many federal benefits including student loans for anyone convicted of a drug offense.” (Alexander 53). With the War on Drugs in full swing at this time and the implementation of Crack cocaine into black communities, it would not be long before communities of color would have to move. Interestingly enough, “HUD policies excluding people from housing assistance based on arrests as well as convictions guarantee highly discriminatory results.” (Alexander, 146). But the U.S. is no foreigner to exclusionary policy. Another piece of preventative legislation was a policy known as redlining. In an article titled The Case for Reparations, author Te-Nehisi Coates describes redlining as a system of rating and prevention that outlined black neighborhoods in red, rating them with the letter D, deeming them too high of a risk for mortgage insurance (Coates, CFR, 2014). The marginalization of black communities took the shape of being “herded into the sights of unscrupulous lenders who took them for money and sport,” (Coates, CFR, 2014), forcing black folks into purchasing “contracts,” a result of the U.S. Congress’ creation of the Federal Housing Association or, (FHA), in 1934. Redlining was forcing capable black men and women, who were able to afford a home, to pay much more than the value of the house for these contracts that were uninsured, high risk, and provided no equity. With the ability to prevent the settlement of colored folk, the U.S. govt. would move on to the next step in their plan.

“Redlining,” says Coates, was “excluding black people from the most legitimate means of obtaining a mortgage” (Coates, CFR, 2014).

Detainment

Slavery was a business, and for the businessmen (who are almost entirely white men), profits and a mass of wealth would be able to be guaranteed and secured, so long as the body performed. At the time, slavery was lucrative, but there were also a lot of bodies to profit from. Author Howard Zinn of A People’s History of the United States writes that “a million blacks had already been brought from Africa to South America and the Caribbean, to the Portuguese and Spanish colonies, to work as slaves” (Zinn 26). Zinn further explains that “Slavery was immensely profitable to some masters” (Zinn, 33). As U.S. policy changed and civil rights movements emerged and flourished, the few who were profiting had to ensure the maintenance of wealth, but how? By having a massive population of people who have historically demonstrated marketability and profitability, detainment would allow for the next part of the business plan to be executed. Alexander writes that “Rich and powerful people, including former vice president dick Cheney, have invested millions in private prisons” (Alexander, 230). With such a significant position of investment in the industry, there would have to be ways to secure capital gains, and the only way to get a return on investment would be to keep prisons filled. So how could that be done? With the U.S. government handcuffing black folks as they simultaneously handed them drugs, and with U.S. policies criminalizing the forced behaviors of communities of color, the system began to collect bodies and lots of them. Having a price on each head and with policies designed to force individuals out of their homes and targeted for arrest, the detainment strategy was effective. With black folks detained, now what?

Relocation

The Great Migration, a topic recently covered in a lecture during a Community and regional Development class taught by Dr. L.T. Winn at the University of California, Davis, describes the U.S. historical event, which comprised of six million African Americans who would flee the South (Winn, Oct.3, 2019). Seeking economic and employment opportunities, protection from the law, and to escape from Jim Crow, they were envisioning a promise land of milk and honey. (Winn, Oct.3, 2019) In trying to evade marginalization and oppression, black communities were merely switching seats on the same sinking ship. With a gaze set upon an illusory destination of freedom, colored folks were often encouraged to stay in school, and that education was a way to secure a bright future. However, upon arriving in education systems across the U.S., communities of color would face the same exclusion and redirection of their bodies, “as well as “zero tolerance” policies that effectively follow youth of color from schools to jails.” (Alexander, 11). The school to prison pipeline, or school to prison Nexus, as Dr. Winn describes in a lecture on the subject, refers to the construction of schools and policies designed for “producing students who are prepared to go to prison. (Winn, Nov. 11, 2019). With black communities secured behind bars, here come the profits. Alexander describes the “the aggressive enforcement” of black communities and the ways in which it self-fullingly creates an “enormous market for convict leasing, in which prisoners were contracted out as laborers to the highest private bidder” (Alexander, 31). In facilities that stripped individuals of everything, not only would the government create a system that communities of color would depend on, but one that could not be escaped from. By being able to move the black community into a series of massive investment portfolios (private prisons), time would need to do its job, and money would continue to flow, which would once again turn the body into a profit.

Conclusion

The U.S. has re-framed their business plan. It is one that continues to profit at the expense of the black community. In The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness, Michelle Alexander shows how the United States’ deployment of mass incarceration, through legislation and the enforcement of detainment and relocation, has reconstructed the commodification of the black body. Where does the Black community turn to address the systematic marginalization and oppression experienced? Are they supposed to turn to the system that, not only is the only one that can help, but the very one that’s harming?
