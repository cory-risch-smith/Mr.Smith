Search Engine Optimization, or SEO, helps to improve the quality of network traffic to the site you host on the internet through natural search engine results. In an article titled, "HTML5 Semantic Tags: What Are They and How To Use Them!", written by Jason Barnard in 2017, talks a lot about this subject and some of the quickest and easiest ways to improve SEO through correcting the HTML semantic markup tags. One thing that peaked my curiosity about this article wasn't that increasing network traffic could bring your business millions it might be losing out on due to poorly written code, not because SEO is a "HOT" trend, no. Disability. Let me Explain.

In his article, Barnard discusses the importance of HTML5 and the proper use of semantic tags so that browsers can more efficiently provide information to the audience, some of whom have disabilitys. People who are vision impaired or blind and utilize screen readers depend on good code, code that is written to support their disability and encourage them to include themselves in this ever evolving realm of technology. If you are looking for a way to improve the ethics of your company, to bring more light to your company's system of values and beliefs, disability rights is a great place to begin and a fantastic way to ensure that not only are you supporting, perhaps, an underserved community within technology, but you are also increasing exposure of your business or product.

One of the things I tried to do in styling a recent blog app was just that, bring a cognisance to the design, and making sure that the layout is simple, straight forward, and easy to navigate. I also tried to make button sizes slightly larger for anyone suffering with movement limitations or impingements, particularly parkinson's, making sure the button is the only thing in its area to be pressed, and that it is not stacked up against a bunch of small hyperlinked text.

In terms of the structural layout, I tried to create a sort of card-feature with the images and text, where they can be easily manipulated and adjusted with CSS, but individually targeted if need be. I also tried to, with the mobile in particular, keep things as simple and minimalist as possible with buttons and animations. I liked the curved border for the pictures, giving it a softer feel to the contrasting color tones.

I think this design is really striving to practice advocation through design, using different design elements, strategies, layouts, focused heavily on supporting the target audience. I think one of the most challenging things about the visual aspect of my design is how much I can over simplify things, versuus how I can just-as easily under-simplify and make the site complicated and crowded. While we all continue to work with one another and grow through time, design is something I constantly visit humbly when looking to see how I can best be of service.
